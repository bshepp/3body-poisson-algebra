§1 Symplectic phase space

Forget shapes in space for a moment. The natural arena for classical mechanics is phase space: every degree of freedom $q$ is paired with a conjugate momentum $p$. A single planar pendulum lives in $(q, p) \in S^1 \times \mathbb R$, and its entire future is a curve on that cylinder — one trajectory per energy.

The Hamiltonian is

$$ H(q, p) \;=\; \tfrac12 p^2 \;-\; \cos q. $$

For three planar bodies the phase space jumps from $\mathbb R^2$ to $\mathbb R^{12}$, but the principle is identical: the state of the universe is a point, and time evolution is a curve.

§2 The Poisson bracket

Phase space comes equipped with an operation that turns two functions of $(q, p)$ into a third:

$$ \{f, g\} \;=\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial q}\,\frac{\partial g}{\partial p} \;-\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial p}\,\frac{\partial g}{\partial q}. $$

This is the Poisson bracket. It is bilinear, antisymmetric, and satisfies the Jacobi identity. Hamilton’s equations are simply $\dot f = \{f, H\}$ — the Hamiltonian uses the bracket to flow every observable forward in time.

Two facts are already visible:

  • $\{q, p\} = 1$ — this is the canonical relation that defines the symplectic structure.
  • $\{H, H\} = 0$ — every Hamiltonian commutes with itself, so energy is conserved.

The bracket of two functions is itself a function. Iterating the bracket on a starting set of observables generates a Lie algebra — and that is exactly what A395423 measures the dimension of.

§5 The dimension sequence

Stack the values for $V(r) = 1/r$ and you get the sequence A395423 is named for:

$$ a(0),\, a(1),\, a(2),\, a(3) \;=\; 3,\; 6,\; 17,\; 116. $$

The remarkable empirical fact is that almost every two-body potential gives the same first four terms. The chart below shows the comparison — only the harmonic potential $V(r) = r^2$ is special, because its algebra closes at level 2.

§6 Universality — the dimension doesn't care

What if you don't know the right potential? Maybe the bodies interact through gravity, or screened gravity, or a logarithmic plasma force. Surely the algebra changes? Slide the knobs below to mix $\,1/r$, $1/r^2$, and $\log r$ contributions:

$$ V(r) \;=\; \alpha\,\tfrac{1}{r} \;+\; \beta\,\tfrac{1}{r^2} \;+\; \gamma\,\log r. $$

§7 Spatial dimension $d \in \{1,2,3\}$

Universality is not just a fact about potentials. It is a fact about geometry. The same Lie algebra grows out of three particles on a line, three particles in a plane, and three particles in space. The phase space changes shape; the algebra does not.

§8 Mass invariance

The early calculations almost ran aground here. With unequal masses a buggy version of SymPy miscounted level 3 as 69 instead of 116, and for a while we believed the dimension did depend on the mass ratios. The fix in SymPy 1.13.3 restored the truth: every choice of positive masses gives the same Lie algebra.

§9 The numerical witness: SVD rank gap

How do we know the dimension is 116 and not 117 or 115? The symbolic generators are lambdify'd to NumPy callables, evaluated at hundreds of random phase-space points, and stacked into a tall matrix $M$. The number of nonzero singular values of $M$ is the dimension of the span. The trick is the gap.

§10 The frontier: $a(4) \geq 5604$

For four bodies the same machinery applies, but the symbolic algebra explodes. Level 0 has 6 generators (one for each pair), level 1 has 14, level 2 has 62, and level 3 climbs to a number we cannot yet compute symbolically. The best lower bound at the time of writing is from a 4096-sample numerical SVD running on AWS spot instances:

$$ a(4) \;\geq\; 5604. $$

Three bodies. One bracket. Four numbers. The rest is open.

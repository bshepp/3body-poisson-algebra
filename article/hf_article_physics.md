# Every Interaction Law Produces the Same Algebra

*A 993-row dataset of exact Lie algebra structure for the classical and quantum N-body problem*

Take three particles. Let them interact via gravity \\(1/r\\), the Calogero-Moser potential \\(1/r^2\\), dipole-dipole coupling \\(1/r^3\\), a logarithmic vortex potential, the Yukawa nuclear force \\(e^{-\mu r}/r\\), or any polynomial \\(r^n\\) with \\(n \ge 4\\). Compute all iterated Poisson brackets of the pairwise interaction Hamiltonians. Count how many are linearly independent at each bracket depth.

You get the same answer every time: **[3, 6, 17, 116]**.

This article introduces [bshepp/pairwise-poisson-algebras](https://huggingface.co/datasets/bshepp/pairwise-poisson-algebras) — the first systematic computation of pairwise Poisson bracket algebras for the N-body problem. The dataset contains **993 rows across 13 Parquet tables**, including exact rational structure constant tensors, dimension sequences for \\(N = 3\\) through \\(N = 50\\), a continuous sweep across 576 potential exponents, Yukawa (screened Coulomb) universality, quantum Moyal deformation data, Bell inequality tests, and representation-theoretic decompositions under \\(S_3\\) and \\(S_4\\).

The headline result is a universality theorem: the algebraic structure generated by pairwise interactions is independent of the interaction law, the masses, the charges, and the spatial dimension. Only three potentials in all of mathematics break this pattern.

> **Note:** A companion article focuses on the **neural-network** half of this dataset — the `neural_algebras` split (21 rows, 12 coupling types, 7 universality classes at L=3) shows that linear neural networks under SGD-with-momentum produce **richer** Poisson algebras than any physical system. See `hf_article.md` in the repository.

## What is a pairwise Poisson algebra?

Given \\(N\\) bodies with positions \\(\mathbf{q}_i\\) and momenta \\(\mathbf{p}_i\\), every pair \\((i,j)\\) has a pairwise Hamiltonian:

$$H_{ij} = \frac{|\mathbf{p}_i - \mathbf{p}_j|^2}{2\mu_{ij}} + q_i q_j \cdot V(|\mathbf{q}_i - \mathbf{q}_j|)$$

The Poisson bracket \\(\lbrace H_{ij}, H_{kl} \rbrace\\) of two pairwise Hamiltonians is a new function on phase space. Bracket that with another \\(H_{mn}\\), and you get yet another function. The **pairwise Poisson algebra** is the Lie algebra generated by closing this process:

$$\mathcal{A}_V = \mathrm{Lie}\bigl(\lbrace H_{ij} : 1 \le i \lt j \le N \rbrace\bigr)$$

This algebra is graded by *bracket depth* (level). Level 0 contains the \\(\binom{N}{2}\\) pairwise Hamiltonians themselves. Level 1 contains all their mutual brackets. Level 2 contains brackets of level-1 elements with level-0 elements, and so on. The **dimension sequence** \\([d_0, d_1, d_2, \ldots]\\) records the cumulative number of linearly independent generators through each level.

For three bodies \\(N=3\\), level 0 gives 3 generators (one per pair). Level 1 adds 3 more (the "tidal competition" terms \\(\lbrace H_{12}, H_{13} \rbrace\\), etc.), for a total of 6. Level 2 contributes 11 more, reaching 17. Level 3 adds 99 more, reaching 116.

The question that drives this entire dataset is: **does the sequence [3, 6, 17, 116] depend on the choice of potential \\(V(r)\\)?**

## The universality result

It does not. Here is the evidence from the dataset:

```python
from datasets import load_dataset
import pandas as pd

ds = load_dataset("bshepp/pairwise-poisson-algebras", "dimension_sequences")
df = ds["train"].to_pandas()

# Filter to N=3, exact computations with L3 data
exact = df[(df["N"] == 3) & (df["is_exact"] == True) & (df["dim_L3"].notna())]
print(exact[["potential", "bracket_type", "dim_L3"]].to_string(index=False))
```

Output (abridged):

```
    potential bracket_type  dim_L3
         1/r      poisson     116
       1/r^2      poisson     116
       1/r^3      poisson     116
       1/r^4      poisson     116
         r^4      poisson     116
         r^5      poisson     116
          ...          ...     ...
         r^10     poisson     116
         log      poisson     116
  composite(...)  poisson     116
     yukawa(...)  poisson     116
         1/r        moyal     117    # quantum: +1
         r^1      poisson       5    # exceptional
         r^2      poisson      15    # exceptional
         r^3      poisson     109    # exceptional
```

Sixteen different potentials — singular and polynomial, power-law, transcendental, and exponentially screened — all produce dimension 116 at level 3. Only three are exceptional:

- **\\(r^1\\) (linear)**: The algebra closes at dimension 5. It is the filiform nilpotent Lie algebra \\(L_{5,2}\\).
- **\\(r^2\\) (harmonic oscillator)**: The algebra closes at dimension 15. This is the only integrable-looking exception, with enhanced SU(d) symmetry.
- **\\(r^3\\) (cubic)**: Dimension 109 at level 3 — seven generators short of 116. The level-3 algebra is qualitatively different: it is not nilpotent, and its lower central series oscillates with period 5.

Everything else — from \\(1/r^{0.02}\\) to \\(r^{10}\\), from logarithmic vortices to Yukawa nuclear potentials — gives exactly [3, 6, 17, 116].

## The exponent landscape

The dataset includes a continuous sweep of 576 exponents: 500 at level 2 and 76 at level 3, covering both \\(1/r^n\\) and \\(r^n\\) families:

```python
ds = load_dataset("bshepp/pairwise-poisson-algebras", "dimension_sequences")
df = ds["train"].to_pandas()

sweep = df[df["source_file"].str.contains("level2_exponent|l3_exponent", na=False, regex=True)]
print(f"Total exponents: {len(sweep)}")
print(f"Universal: {(sweep['dim_L2'] == 17).sum()}")
```

At level 2, out of 500 values only two deviate: \\(r^1\\) and \\(r^2\\), at the exact integers. The transition is sharp — \\(r^{0.98}\\) gives 17, \\(r^{1.0}\\) gives 15, \\(r^{1.02}\\) gives 17 again. The harmonic oscillator is a measure-zero island of special behavior in an ocean of universality.

At level 3, the picture sharpens further. The \\(r^2\\) harmonic point produces a symmetric V-shaped dip: \\(\dim(L_3)\\) descends \\(116 \to 108 \to 87\\) as \\(n \to 2^-\\) and climbs back \\(87 \to 108 \to 116\\) as \\(n \to 2^+\\), with the classical \\(r^2\\) value at \\(\dim = 15\\) at the exact center.

## It does not depend on the masses

The dataset contains a mass ratio sweep from \\(m_3/m_1 = 0.001\\) to \\(10^{10}\\):


## It does not depend on the charges

The `charge_sensitivity` split includes 38 configurations. A systematic sweep of the \\((+1, +q, -1)\\) family for \\(q = 1, 2, \ldots, 20\\) using exact symbolic rank over \\(\mathbb{Q}\\) resolved earlier ambiguities: **all 20 give [3, 6, 17, 116]**. The two earlier departures (Li+ at 111, H2+ at 115) arose from specific mass-charge combinations (physical masses like 1836 or 12789), not from the charge geometry alone.

## It does not depend on the spatial dimension

The dimension sequence [3, 6, 17, 116] is identical at \\(d = 1\\) (particles on a line), \\(d = 2\\) (planar), and \\(d = 3\\) (spatial), tested for both \\(N = 3\\) and \\(N = 4\\). The algebra sees only the combinatorial structure of the interaction graph \\(K_N\\), not the ambient geometry.

## Yukawa: universality beyond power laws

The Yukawa potential \\(V(r) = e^{-\mu r}/r\\) — the screened Coulomb interaction governing nuclear forces — is the first non-power-law singular potential tested. Using a Taylor-expansion composite representation, the dataset confirms universality for 6 screening parameters \\(\mu = 0.1\\) to \\(5.0\\) and 3 physical nuclear systems:

```python
ds = load_dataset("bshepp/pairwise-poisson-algebras", "physical_systems")
df = ds["train"].to_pandas()
nuclear = df[df["category"].isin(["nuclear", "plasma"])]
print(nuclear[["system_label", "matches_universal"]].to_string(index=False))
```

```
    system_label  matches_universal
    Tritium/He-3               True
   Dusty Plasma               True
          p-n-n               True
```

This extends universality from power-law singularities \\(1/r^n\\) and transcendental singularities \\(\log r\\) to exponentially screened potentials. The shared mechanism is the universal cubic chain rule \\(du/dq \sim u^3\\) arising from the substitution \\(u = 1/r\\).

## Quantum deformation adds exactly one dimension

Replacing the Poisson bracket with the Moyal bracket, the quantum commutator \\([\hat{A}, \hat{B}]/(i\hbar)\\), yields [3, 6, 17, **117**] — exactly one extra generator at level 3. The dataset contains 12 quantum rows that establish a complete classification:

| Potential class | Classical dim(L3) | Quantum dim(L3) | Growth |
|---|---|---|---|
| Singular: \\(1/r^n\\), log, composites | 116 | 117 | +1 |
| Polynomial: \\(r^n\\), \\(n \ge 4\\) | 116 | 116 | 0 |
| GUE composite: log + \\(r^2\\) | 116 | 116 | 0 (harmonic suppresses) |
| Pure log | 116 | 117 | +1 |
| Exceptional: \\(r^1\\), \\(r^2\\), \\(r^3\\) | 5 / 15 / 109 | 5 / 15 / 109 | 0 |

The pure log result resolves a GUE ambiguity: the Dyson log-gas (which includes harmonic confinement) does not grow under quantization, but the pure logarithmic potential does. The harmonic component \\(r^2\\) suppresses quantum deformation.

## Structure constant tensors

The dataset contains 16 exact rational structure constant tensors — the full \\(C^k_{ij}\\) satisfying \\([e_i, e_j] = \sum_k C^k_{ij} e_k\\). These are \\(17 \times 17 \times 17\\) tensors with entries in \\(\mathbb{Q}\\).

```python
import json
import numpy as np

ds = load_dataset("bshepp/pairwise-poisson-algebras", "structure_constants")
df = ds["train"].to_pandas()

for _, row in df.iterrows():
    C = np.array(json.loads(row["structure_constants"]), dtype=float)
    nz = np.count_nonzero(C)
    print(f"{row['potential']:>20s}  dim={row['algebra_dim']:>3d}  "
          f"nonzero={nz:>5d}  Killing=({row['killing_signature']})")
```

A key finding: **all 12 non-harmonic 17-dimensional algebras are isomorphic**. They share identical Killing eigenvalues, adjoint-rank multisets, Casimir traces, and canonical basis orderings. The universality is not just dimensional — it is structural. These 12 potentials produce literally the *same* Lie algebra, up to basis change.

## Scaling with N

For \\(N \ge 4\\) bodies, the dataset contains exact dimension sequences through level 2 for \\(N = 3\\) through \\(9\\), and level-0/level-1 data through \\(N = 50\\). The scaling formulas are:

$$L_0(N) = \binom{N}{2}, \qquad L_1(N) = \frac{N(3N-5)}{2}, \qquad L_2(N) = \frac{N(4N^2 - 9N + 3)}{2} \quad (N \ge 4)$$

The `scaling_formulas` split documents a meta-scientific narrative: the original \\(L_2\\) formula, fitted from \\(N = 3, 4, 5, 6\\), was falsified when \\(N = 7\\) and \\(N = 8\\) data became available. The corrected formula emerged from a graph-theoretic decomposition:

- new generators at level 0 = \\(\binom{N}{2}\\) = edges of \\(K_N\\)
- new generators at level 1 = \\(N(N-2)\\) = wedges
- new generators at level 2 = \\(12\binom{N}{3}\\) = 12 per triangle of \\(K_N\\)

This suggests new generators at level \\(k\\) scale as \\(f(k) \cdot \binom{N}{k+1}\\), with the algebra "seeing" \\((k+1)\\)-clique subgraphs of the complete interaction graph at each bracket depth.

At \\(N = 4\\), the level-3 sequence is [6, 14, 62, 1260] — yielding 1198 new generators at level 3. The prediction for \\(N = 5\\) is \\(L_3 = 6135\\) if the pattern holds, but this computation requires ranking a 1.1 million x 760,000 matrix over \\(\mathbb{Q}\\) and has so far exceeded 256 GB of RAM.

## The GUE connection

The Dyson log-gas — the classical Hamiltonian system whose Boltzmann weight gives the GUE eigenvalue distribution — has pairwise Hamiltonian:

$$H_{ij}^{\text{GUE}} = \frac{(p_i - p_j)^2}{4} - 2\log|q_i - q_j|$$

This maps directly to our framework. The result: **[3, 6, 17, 116]**, identical to Newtonian gravity.

The Montgomery-Odlyzko law tells us that Riemann zeta zeros have GUE statistics. The Poisson algebra governing those statistics is the same algebra that governs gravity. This does not prove anything about the Riemann Hypothesis, but it places the algebraic structure underlying prime number correlations in the same universality class as celestial mechanics.

## The three exceptions

### \\(r^1\\): the filiform algebra \\(L_{5,2}\\)

The linear potential \\(V = r\\) produces an algebra of dimension 5 that closes after level 2. It is identified as the 5-dimensional filiform nilpotent Lie algebra \\(L_{5,2}\\), with Killing signature (3+, 0-, 2 zero) and solvable length 2.

### \\(r^2\\): the harmonic oscillator

The quadratic potential \\(V = r^2\\) produces dimension 15 and is the only potential yielding a finite algebra that is not nilpotent. Its enhanced dynamical symmetry (the Fradkin tensor / SU(d) action) generates additional relations that collapse the algebra. Despite being "integrable" in the classical sense, the Calogero-Moser system \\(1/r^2\\), also integrable, does *not* collapse — it gives the full 116.

### \\(r^3\\): the non-nilpotent anomaly

The cubic potential matches universal behavior through level 2 (dim 17, identical invariants), then diverges at level 3 (dim 109 instead of 116). The level-3 algebra is qualitatively different from all others in the catalog:

- It is **not nilpotent** — the lower central series oscillates with period 5: dims [52, 5, 10, 65, 93, 52, 5, ...]
- Solvable length increases from 3 to 4
- The center is 80-dimensional (80/109)

Whether this algebra is a known algebraic structure or genuinely new is an open question that may interest pure algebraists.

## Bell tests and contextuality

Two additional splits probe the quantum-information structure of these algebras:

**CHSH Bell tests** (`bell_test`, 9 rows): Three phase-space strata (equilateral, pair-apparatus, separated) with three observable variants each. The tidal competition generators \\(K_i = \lbrace H_{ij}, H_{ik} \rbrace\\) carry the strongest inter-body correlations with max \\(|S| = 1.77\\) at equilateral, but the CHSH bound \\(|S| \lt 2\\) is never violated. The Poisson algebra is classical in the Bell sense.

**Contextuality tests** (`contextuality`, 16 rows): All 16 tested algebras (dimensions 5 to 109) have **zero commuting pairs**. Every pair of generators has a non-zero Poisson bracket. The orthogonality graph is empty, KS coloring is trivially satisfiable, and Peres-Mermin squares cannot be constructed. The algebra is maximally non-commutative — a stark contrast with quantum operator algebras.

## N=4 atlas: first four-body data

The `spectral_statistics` split includes the first atlas data for \\(N = 4\\): three 1D slices through the four-body shape space (300 total points, 500 samples each), all showing rank 62 with no drops. This includes the equal-spacing slice with bodies at \\(0, d, 2d, 3d\\), which passes through configurations with enhanced permutation symmetry yet shows no rank reduction.

For \\(N = 3\\), rank drops occur at Lagrange (equilateral, \\(S_3\\) fixed point), Euler (collinear, \\(\mathbb{Z}_2\\)), and along isosceles curves — all fixed-point sets of \\(S_3\\). The absence of drops for \\(N = 4\\) along 1D collinear slices may indicate that rank drops require the full 2D/3D shape space to manifest.

The `tier_decomposition` split (40 rows) shows how the \\(N = 4\\) candidate generator space decomposes under \\(S_4\\): 23,226 candidates but only 1,260 independent (94.6% syzygies, vs 25.6% for \\(N = 3\\)). The dominant irreps are standard (37.4%) and sign-standard (37.9%).

## What this dataset is for

This dataset is designed for three audiences:

1. **Mathematical physicists** studying integrability, symmetry algebras, and the N-body problem. The universality result constrains any theory that tries to distinguish integrable from non-integrable systems using pairwise algebraic structure alone — the exactly integrable Calogero-Moser system produces the same algebra as non-integrable gravity.

2. **Algebraists** interested in infinite-dimensional Lie algebras, structure constants, and nilpotent algebras. The 16 exact rational tensors are, to our knowledge, the first published structure constants for any 116-dimensional Poisson algebra. The \\(r^3\\) non-nilpotent algebra with its oscillating lower central series may be a new algebraic object.

3. **ML researchers** working on symbolic regression, equation discovery, or mathematical conjecture generation. The scaling formulas, the conjecture-falsification-correction narrative in the `scaling_formulas` split, and the rich structure in the exponent sweep data are natural targets for automated discovery methods.

## Open problems

Several computations remain beyond current reach:

- **N=5 level 3**: The prediction is \\(L_3(5) = 6135\\). Computing this requires ranking a matrix with over a trillion rational entries. Modular arithmetic, rank over \\(\mathrm{GF}(p)\\) for several primes, could make this feasible.

- **Level-3 isomorphism**: We proved isomorphism at level 2 (dim 17). Does it extend to level 3 (dim 116)? This requires computing at least two level-3 structure constant tensors — each one a \\(116^3 \approx 1.6\\) million entry rational tensor.

- **\\(r^3\\) algebraic classification**: Is the 109-dimensional non-nilpotent \\(r^3\\) algebra a known object, or is it new? Its oscillating lower central series with period 5 is unusual.

- **N=4 level-3 universality**: Does \\(L_3(4) = 1260\\) hold for \\(1/r^2\\), \\(1/r^3\\), log? Three feasible computations that would test potential universality at \\(N = 4\\).

If any of these interest you, the data and code are open. We would welcome collaborators.
